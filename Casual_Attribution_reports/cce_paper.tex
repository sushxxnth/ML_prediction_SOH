\section{Introduction}

%\subsection{The battery degradation problem}

\textcolor{red}{Lithium-ion batteries power the global transition to electric mobility, but understanding and mitigating their complex degradation trajectories remains a central challenge \citep{hua2024review}. Current battery management systems (BMS) in electric vehicles treat capacity fade as a black box they relay the current state of health but cannot diagnose the underlying electrochemical causes or prescribe life-extending operational changes \citep{li2025advanced}.} Battery packs account for 30--40\% of electric vehicle cost \citep{ziegler2021determinants}, and even a 10\% drop in usable capacity can reduce resale value by 20--30\% \citep{saxena2019quantifying}. \textcolor{red}{Without actionable insight, premature replacements cost the industry billions of dollars annually \citep{harper2019recycling}.}

\textcolor{red}{Degradation emerges from competing electrochemical pathways, primarily governed by temperature, state of charge (SOC), and cycling rates \citep{hua2024review}.} The solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer, which forms on the anode during initial cycles, continues to grow throughout the battery's life, consuming cyclable lithium \citep{peled1979electrochemical}. \textcolor{red}{At sub-optimal temperatures or extreme charging rates, lithium plating and mechanical stress from active material cracking severely accelerate irreversible capacity loss \citep{li2025advanced}.} Understanding which mechanism dominates in a given situation is essential for targeted intervention, but current BMS systems do not provide this information.

Electric vehicles spend more than 90\% of their operational time parked, \textcolor{red}{making calendar aging driven heavily by storage SOC and resting temperature a dominant factor in real-world capacity fade \citep{hua2024review}.} \textcolor{red}{However, the vast majority of commercial development still disproportionately focuses on active driving cycles \citep{li2025advanced}.} Field studies have repeatedly shown that calendar aging can account for 60--80\% of total capacity loss in moderate-use vehicles \citep{saxena2019quantifying}, yet this finding has not translated into widespread changes in BMS design. The practical implications are substantial, as users following dynamically optimized storage guidelines could significantly extend their battery's useful life.

%\subsection{Limitations of existing approaches}

Two modeling paradigms have dominated battery health estimation, each with significant limitations.
\textit{Physics-based models}, including the Doyle-Fuller-Newman formulation \citep{doyle1993modeling} and its variants \citep{plett2015battery}, describe electrochemical processes from first principles and can distinguish between degradation modes. However, they require extensive parameterization for each battery chemistry, involve computational costs unsuitable for embedded BMS, and demand calibration data that may not be available for production cells.
\textcolor{red}{Data-driven models have rapidly evolved from simple regressions to massive foundation models. Most recently, state-of-the-art architectures like Temporal Fusion Transformers \citep{ma2024battery} and large-scale pre-trained language models for zero-shot chemistry transfer \citep{ren2024pretrained} have drastically improved remaining useful life predictability. However, despite their scaling properties, these models continue to operate as strict electrochemical black boxes. Explainable AI (XAI) techniques, such as SHAP analysis \citep{fan2023interpretable} and inherently interpretable attention mechanisms \citep{wu2024inherent}, attempt to bridge this gap via feature importance rankings, but they fundamentally cannot map statistical data points backwards to the underlying Arrhenius kinetics or Tafel equations that physically generated them.}

Recent hybrid and physics-aware models address these limitations by imposing dynamic constraints. Hybrid physics-ML models \citep{li2022physics, thelen2022integrating, ashraf2024dinn} and digital twin architectures \citep{li2024digitaltwin} achieve high-fidelity state tracking, while differentiable modeling enables optimal fast charging \citep{zhou2023differentiable}. Yet, a critical capability gap remains: none of these systems simultaneously provide real-time causal mechanism discovery \citep{runge2023causal} and counterfactual intervention evaluation \citep{park2023socmanagement}. As a result, current systems can forecast \textit{when} a battery will fail and output \textit{which} model feature was significant, but they cannot explain \textit{why} specific degradation mechanisms (e.g., SEI growth vs. Lithium plating) dominate or provide prescriptive, actionable recommendations for life extension.

\subsection{Data-driven SOH and RUL prediction:}
Machine learning has become the dominant paradigm for battery health estimation. Early approaches used LSTMs to estimate state of charge from voltage--current sequences \citep{chemali2017long}, while CNNs extracted spatial features from charging curves for capacity estimation \citep{shen2019deep}. More recently, transformer architectures have been applied to remaining useful life (RUL) prediction \citep{chen2022transformer, park2025transformer} and state-of-charge estimation with physical constraints \citep{pitm2025}, leveraging self-attention to capture long-range dependencies in degradation trajectories. Severson et al.\ \citep{severson2019nature} showed that early-cycle discharge features can predict battery lifetime, and Roman et al.\ \citep{roman2021machine} systematized this into end-to-end pipelines. Several approaches have improved generalization: transfer learning \citep{liu2023transfer, che2022battery, zhu2022transfer}, partial least-squares regression for cross-chemistry RUL estimation \citep{wang2024generalizable}, temperature-aware early-cycle features \citep{sugiarto2025temperature}, and multimodal fusion of impedance and capacity data \citep{chen2026multimodal, hu2023eis}. Closed-loop optimization has also been applied to fast-charging protocol design \citep{attia2020closedloop}. \textcolor{red}{Most recently, foundation models have sought to unify degradation prediction across datasets. \citet{ma2024battery} established strong baselines for remaining useful life prediction using Temporal Fusion Transformers, while \citet{ren2024pretrained} demonstrated zero-shot cross-chemistry transfer via pre-trained battery language models.} Similarly, \citet{zhao2024gpt4battery} proposed GPT4Battery, an LLM-based framework that adapts a pre-trained GPT-2 model to zero-shot SOH estimation across diverse battery chemistries. Unlike our retrieval-augmented approach, GPT4Battery transfers across chemistries through statistical language model adaptation rather than physics-based features, limiting its interpretability and causal grounding. Despite their predictive accuracy, these methods operate as black boxes: they forecast \textit{when} a battery will fail but cannot explain \textit{why} it is degrading or \textit{what} the user can do about it. \textcolor{red}{Park et al.\ \citep{park2025transformer} demonstrated that while attention weights can effectively highlight critical degradation features across diverse cells, they still function as a statistical proxy rather than true physical mechanism attribution.}

\subsection{Physics-informed and hybrid approaches:}
Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) \citep{raissi2019physics} embed governing equations as soft constraints in the training loss, enabling neural networks to respect physical laws while retaining the flexibility of data-driven models. In the battery domain, PINNs have been used for electrode-level state estimation \citep{li2022physics} and degradation prognosis \citep{wang2024pinn}. \textcolor{red}{For instance, \citet{wang2024pinn} deployed pure PINNs for real-time SOH estimation from partial charge curves, while recent cycle-aware frameworks explicitly distinguish between active cycling and calendar aging via physics constraints \citep{cycleaware2025}.} Hybrid frameworks that couple electrochemical simulations with neural network predictions have shown improved accuracy and interpretability \citep{thelen2022integrating, dawsonelli2018datasci}. \textcolor{red}{Additionally, physics-aware attentive networks \citep{tcn2025} fuse equivalent circuit model features with temporal convolutions to stabilize state estimation under varying temperatures.} Most recently, Gu et al.\ \citep{gu2026realtime} developed P-PINNSPM, a parameterized PINN trained over the aging-related parameter space of the Single Particle Model. By identifying changes in electrode active material volume fractions from tail-end charging voltage segments, their approach achieves a 47$\times$ speedup over numerical solvers and improves SOH estimation accuracy by over 60\% compared to parameter-free models. The identified parameters also enable profile-agnostic estimation and extrapolation to unseen degradation states. However, P-PINNSPM operates at the parameter level identifying \textit{that} diffusion coefficients or volume fractions have changed without attributing these changes to specific electrochemical degradation mechanisms such as SEI growth or lithium plating.

\subsection{Degradation diagnostics and mechanism identification:}
Understanding which degradation mechanism dominates under given operating conditions is essential for targeted intervention. Incremental capacity analysis \citep{bloom2005differential} and diagnostic models \citep{dubarry2017synthesize, birkl2017degradation} can distinguish between loss of lithium inventory and loss of active material from post-mortem or slow-rate cycling data. Comprehensive reviews \citep{vetter2005ageing, edge2021lithium} have catalogued the major mechanisms SEI growth, lithium plating, active material loss, and electrolyte decomposition and their dependence on temperature, state of charge, and C-rate. Calendar aging studies \citep{schimpe2018comprehensive, ecker2014calendar, keil2016calendar, wright2002calendar} have quantified time-dependent degradation under storage conditions. However, these diagnostic approaches typically require either destructive testing, specialized low-rate cycling protocols, or offline analysis, making them unsuitable for real-time deployment in battery management systems. \textcolor{red}{Recently, \citet{wu2024inherent} introduced inherently interpretable machine learning using SHAP values and attention mechanisms to rank degradation pathways. While this provides feature-level explainability without destructive testing, statistical correlation still lacks the strict causal guarantees provided by bounded physical equations.}


The existing literature showcase strong tools for either predicting health (data-driven) or understanding mechanisms (physics-based), but not both simultaneously in real time. LLM-based frameworks such as GPT4Battery \citep{zhao2024gpt4battery} achieve zero-shot chemistry transfer through statistical adaptation but provide no mechanistic attribution or actionable intervention guidance. Parameter identification approaches \citep{gu2026realtime} reveal internal state changes without mechanism attribution. Diagnostic approaches \citep{birkl2017degradation, dubarry2017synthesize} identify mechanisms but require offline analysis. Data-driven predictors \citep{severson2019nature, roman2021machine} forecast failure without causal explanation. No existing framework combines retrieval-augmented SOH prediction, physics-constrained mechanism attribution, and counterfactual intervention optimization in a unified system. Our work addresses this gap by extending the PINN paradigm from parameter identification to causal mechanism attribution, decomposing capacity loss into five distinct electrochemical pathways and translating diagnoses into actionable user recommendations. \textcolor{red}{Table~\ref{tab:sota_comparison} summarizes this capability gap by comparing our unified framework against recent state-of-the-art baselines across key dimensions of predictability, attribution, and interventional optimization.}


To address the above limitations, we present an integrated framework that uses the following three novel components working together.

\begin{enumerate}[(\alph*)]
    \item {Hybrid Estimation via Retrieval Optimization (HERO):} To obtain the retrieval-augmented prediction, we store 3,979 historical degradation trajectories in a memory bank and retrieve similar cases during inference. Combined with chemistry-invariant lithium inventory features, this achieves 55\% reduction in RUL prediction error on unseen chemistries, a true zero-shot generalization without fine-tuning.
    
    \item {Physics-Constrained Causal Attribution:} A multi-head neural network decomposes capacity loss into five electrochemical mechanisms, constrained by mass conservation and physics priors encoding known degradation thermodynamics. We systematically compare pure Physics-Informed Neural Networks (60.0\% accuracy), boundary-aware variants (77.3\%), and a hybrid architecture integrating expert domain priors (96.0\% accuracy). Validation across 75 scenarios from five independent datasets demonstrates that expert priors contribute 18.7 percentage points beyond pure physics approaches, with 100\% detection of safety-critical lithium plating and collector corrosion mechanisms.

     \item {Counterfactual Optimization:} Using the learned causal model, we simulate alternative operating conditions to predict how degradation mechanisms would change under what if scenarios. This enables optimal short term interventions by evaluating potential interventions before implementation. Validated on NASA and XJTU battery datasets, the counterfactual optimizer achieves 34.6\% average mechanism reduction, with complete elimination of lithium plating in low-temperature scenarios.
    
    
    \item {User-Centric Advisory:} We translate mechanism diagnoses into actionable recommendations, including storage SOC optimization, charging protocol adjustments, and trip planning that balances range with battery health. We validate the advisory system on real-world battery data from NASA Ames (34 cells, 5,609 cycling samples), Stanford Calendar Aging (60 cells spanning up to 69 months of storage), and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy measurements from 94 cells across temperatures ranging from negative 40 °C to positive 50 °C.
\end{enumerate}

The key insight is that these components are individually insufficient but collectively transformative. Accurate prediction enables reliable diagnosis, diagnosis enables targeted intervention and intervention delivers measurable life extension. This integration represents a shift from passive battery monitoring to active battery management. Collectively, we validate our framework across 9 independent public datasets, spanning 4 battery chemistries (LCO, NCM, NCA, LFP), temperatures from $-$40\textdegree{}C to 50\textdegree{}C, and over 11,000 degradation samples. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has jointly addressed causal mechanism attribution, counterfactual intervention optimization, zero-shot cross-chemistry transfer, and context-aware advisory in a single validated framework at this scale.

% \paragraph{Stage 2 (HERO Prediction Engine)}

% The core prediction component operates on a fundamentally different principle than traditional battery models. Instead of trying to memorize degradation patterns from scratch, it maintains a searchable memory of what has happened to similar batteries in the past. % Rather than simply averaging those five historical cases, the system uses a cross-attention mechanism (borrowed from modern natural language processing) to intelligently weight how much to trust each retrieved case. If the current battery's recent behavior closely matches a historical battery that later failed from lithium plating, the system assigns high weight to that case's future trajectory. This retrieval-augmented approach explains why HERO achieves 55\% error reduction compared to baseline methods although it never encounters a truly novel degradation pattern because it can find similar precedents in the database.

% The output from HERO is twofold: a predicted SOH (e.g., 85\% remaining capacity) and a predicted RUL (e.g., 120 cycles until end-of-life at 80\% threshold). % Technical Details of Profile Generation and Weight Computation are described next. The memory bank profiles are created by encoding each historical battery's 50-cycle voltage-current trajectory through a bidirectional GRU with 2 layers and 256 hidden units, producing a 128-dimensional latent vector. During inference, the query battery's recent history is encoded identically and L2-normalized. Cosine similarity identifies the k=5 (or k=3 for zero-shot scenarios) nearest neighbors from the 3,979-trajectory database. Rather than simple averaging, a multi-head cross-attention mechanism (8 heads, following transformer architecture) computes attention weights that determine how much to trust each retrieved case. Batteries with degradation patterns closely matching the query receive higher weights (approaching 1.0), while dissimilar cases receive near-zero weights. These softmax-normalized weights are then used to blend the retrieved trajectories' future predictions. The complete mathematical formulation and ablation studies are detailed in subsequent sections.

% \paragraph{Stage 4 (Causal Attribution via Hybrid PINN)}

% Once we know how much capacity has degraded and whether degradation is accelerating, the next step is to evaluate the reasons for the same. For this purpose, the Hybrid Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) is used. Unlike the previous components that treat degradation as a single number, the PINN decomposes observed capacity loss into contributions from five specific electrochemical mechanisms.

% The architecture uses five separate output heads, one for each mechanism: SEI layer growth, lithium plating, active material loss, electrolyte decomposition, and current collector corrosion. Each head incorporates physics-informed priors through relevant equations. SEI growth is modeled using $Q_{\text{SEI}} = k_{\text{SEI}} \sqrt{t} \exp(-E_a/2RT)$, capturing the diffusion-limited nature of interphase formation where the activation energy $E_a$ is constrained to 35-60 kJ/mol based on literature values. Lithium plating uses simplified Butler-Volmer kinetics with temperature-dependent overpotential, where plating risk increases dramatically below 5°C and at high C-rates. Active material loss follows power-law mechanical fatigue scaling: $Q_{\text{AM}} = k \cdot C_{\text{rate}}^\beta \cdot N^\gamma$, where $\beta$ (1.0-2.0) and $\gamma$ (0.3-1.0) are learned from data to capture the relationship between cycling stress and particle cracking. Electrolyte decomposition incorporates physics priors for solvent oxidation and reduction reactions using $Q_{\text{elec}} = k_{\text{elec}} \cdot \text{SOC}^\alpha \cdot \exp(-E_{\text{elec}}/RT)$, where the exponential dependence on state-of-charge reflects increased decomposition at high voltages, with $\alpha$ typically ranging from 1.5-2.5. Current collector corrosion is modeled with physics priors as $Q_{\text{corr}} = k_{\text{corr}} \cdot \exp(E_{\text{corr}}/RT) \cdot t$, representing the thermally activated oxidation of aluminum or copper current collectors at elevated temperatures and voltages. All five mechanisms benefit from physics-informed constraints that encode domain knowledge while allowing data-driven refinement of parameters.

% The critical innovation is the hybrid approach. Pure physics-informed models struggle in ambiguous cases where multiple mechanisms could explain the observed degradation. Pure data-driven models lack interpretability and fail to generalize. The Hybrid PINN combines the above two approaches. It learns from the physics equations but incorporates domain expert rules to handle boundary cases. For example, when temperature is moderate and C-rate is low, SEI typically dominates. This expert knowledge becomes a soft prior in the loss function.

% Trained on 75 carefully constructed benchmark scenarios spanning five datasets, the Hybrid PINN achieves 96.0\% attribution accuracy. Critically, it reaches 100\% detection on safety-critical mechanisms (lithium plating and corrosion), meaning it never misses a dangerous condition (i.e., lithium plating, which can lead to thermal runaway, and collector corrosion, which can cause sudden cell failure). The output is represented as a percentage breakdown among the five degradation mechanisms described above (i.e., capacity loss is 60\% from lithium plating, 25\% from SEI growth, 10\% from active material loss, 3\% from electrolyte loss, and 2\% from corrosion).

\section{Methodology}
The system integrates four distinct yet interconnected computational components that work together (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}). First, a retrieval-augmented prediction engine (HERO) that uses transformer-based cross-attention mechanisms to intelligently fuse similar historical battery trajectories with current observations. Second, a physics-informed neural network that decomposes degradation into specific electrochemical mechanisms using learned physics constraints. Third, a counterfactual intervention framework that simulates alternative operating conditions to predict how degradation mechanisms would change under "what if" scenarios, enabling optimal short-term interventions. Fourth, a Physics-Aware Temporal Transformer (PATT) that classifies usage patterns and generates context-appropriate recommendations. This hybrid architecture enables both interpretability through physics grounding and adaptability through learned attention mechanisms.


\subsection{HERO: Retrieval-Augmented Prediction and Early Warnign System}
The core prediction component of HERO operates on a fundamentally different principle than traditional battery models. Instead of trying to memorize degradation patterns from scratch, it maintains a searchable memory of what has happened to similar batteries in the past. This memory bank contains 3,979 complete degradation trajectories collected from 76 different battery cells across five independent datasets: NASA Ames (various chemistries and temperatures), CALCE (different manufacturers), Oxford (high-precision degradation tracking), TJU (1C cycling data), and XJTU (high C-rate stress tests).

When a new battery measurement arrives, the system first encodes the recent voltage-current history into a compact mathematical representation (a 128-dimensional vector). This is similar to creating a fingerprint of how the battery has been behaving over the past 50 cycles. It then searches the memory bank for the five most similar historical fingerprints using cosine similarity essentially asking which batteries from our database degraded in a similar pattern.
Rather than simply averaging those five historical cases, the system uses a cross-attention mechanism (borrowed from modern natural language processing) to intelligently weight how much to trust each retrieved case.

The output from HERO is twofold: a predicted SOH (e.g., 85\% remaining capacity) and a predicted RUL (e.g., 120 cycles until end-of-life at 80\% threshold). These predictions carry uncertainty bounds computed via bootstrap sampling, which becomes critical for safety-critical applications where confidence intervals matter. Further, parallel to the main prediction pipeline, a slope-based detector continuously monitors whether degradation is accelerating. The mathematics is straightforward. It fits a linear regression over the most recent 20 cycles to estimate $\frac{d(\text{SOH})}{d(\text{cycle})}$, a current rate of capacity loss. Most batteries degrade gradually at 0.05-0.10\% per cycle. When this slope exceeds 0.17\% per cycle, a threshold optimized on 34 NASA cells, triggers an alert.


The algorithmic implementation of sub-components of HERO module are described as given below.  
\subsubsection{Transformer Attention Mechanism: Mathematical Foundation}

Before describing our battery-specific architecture, we first establish the mathematical foundation borrowed from transformer models \citep{vaswani2017attention}. The core innovation of transformers is the self-attention mechanism, which allows the model to weigh the importance of different input elements when producing an output. This has proven remarkably effective in natural language processing and, as we demonstrate, extends naturally to time-series battery data.

The standard transformer attention mechanism operates on three learned linear projections of the input: the \textbf{query} ($\mathbf{Q}$), \textbf{key} ($\mathbf{K}$), and \textbf{value} ($\mathbf{V}$). Given an input sequence $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ representing $n$ elements (e.g., time steps, tokens, or in our case, battery trajectories) each with dimension $d$, we compute:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_Q, \quad \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_K, \quad \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_V
\label{eq:transformer_projections}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}_Q, \mathbf{W}_K, \mathbf{W}_V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_k}$ are learned weight matrices that project the input into query, key, and value representations, and $d_k$ is the dimension of each head (typically $d_k = d/h$ where $h$ is the number of attention heads).

The attention weights are computed via scaled dot-product attention:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}
\label{eq:scaled_attention}
\end{equation}

The intuition is straightforward. The dot product $\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T$ measures similarity between queries and keys, producing an $n \times n$ attention matrix where element $(i,j)$ indicates how much position $i$ should attend to position $j$. The scaling factor $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ prevents the dot products from growing too large (which would push the softmax into regions with vanishing gradients), and the softmax normalizes these scores into a probability distribution. Finally, these attention weights are used to compute a weighted sum of the value vectors $\mathbf{V}$.

For multi-head attention with $h$ heads, we compute $h$ independent attention functions in parallel:

\begin{equation}
\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{W}_Q^i, \mathbf{K}\mathbf{W}_K^i, \mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}_V^i)
\label{eq:multihead_individual}
\end{equation}

and concatenate their outputs before applying a final linear transformation:

\begin{equation}
\text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)\mathbf{W}_O
\label{eq:multihead_concat}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}_O \in \mathbb{R}^{hd_v \times d}$ projects the concatenated heads back to the original dimension. Multiple heads allow the model to attend to different representation subspaces simultaneously—for example, one head might focus on short-term patterns while another captures long-term trends.

In our application, we use \textit{cross-attention} rather than self-attention. The query $\mathbf{Q}$ comes from the current battery's recent trajectory (the battery we want to predict), while the keys $\mathbf{K}$ and values $\mathbf{V}$ come from the memory bank of historical degradation trajectories. This allows the model to ask: ``which historical batteries degraded similarly to my current battery, and how did they evolve?'' The attention weights determine how much to trust each historical case, and the weighted value vectors produce the final prediction. We use $h=8$ heads following standard practice, enabling the model to simultaneously attend to multiple aspects of similarity (e.g., voltage patterns, temperature history, C-rate profiles).

\subsubsection{Memory Bank Architecture}


The memory bank contains 3,979 degradation trajectory snapshots from 76 battery cells spanning multiple chemistries (Lithium cobalt oxide (LCO), nickel manganese cobalt oxide (NCM), nickel cobalt aluminum oxide (NCA), and lithium iron phosphate (LFP)) and C-rates. The dataset composition reflects samples from the NASA battery dataset \citep{saha2007battery}, CALCE dataset \citep{he2011prognostics}, Oxford dataset \citep{birkl2017oxford}, TJU NCM/NCA dataset (274 samples at 1C), and XJTU NCM dataset (889 samples at 2C-3C charging rates).

Each memory bank entry stores three things: a 128-dimensional latent vector representing the compressed trajectory, the subsequent health evolution (SOH and RUL values), and the environmental context vector. The total storage requirement is approximately 1.3 MB, enabling deployment on resource-constrained edge devices.

\subsubsection{Dynamic Retrieval and Attention Fusion}

At inference time, we encode the battery's recent history into a query vector using a bidirectional GRU \citep{cho2014learning} involving two layers, 256 hidden units. This compresses 50 cycles of voltage and current data into a 128-dimensional vector that we L2-normalize so cosine similarity works properly. Cosine similarity computes the angle between two vectors: $\text{sim}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{m}) = \frac{\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{m}}{\|\mathbf{q}\| \|\mathbf{m}\|}$, where L2-normalization ensures $\|\mathbf{q}\| = \|\mathbf{m}\| = 1$, simplifying this to the dot product $\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{m}$. This measures pattern similarity independent of magnitude, so a battery degrading from 100\% to 90\% can match another degrading from 90\% to 80\% if the shapes are similar.

We then search for the k nearest neighbors in our memory bank. For most cases k=5 works well, though we use k = 3 for zero-shot transfer to completely new chemistries (ablation showed this sweet spot). One trick that helps with calendar aging is we optionally filter retrieved cases to those within 5\% of the current SOH. If fewer than 5 trajectories pass this filter, we relax the condition to 10\%. This ensures we're comparing batteries at similar degradation stages, not mixing fresh cells with end-of-life ones.

The fusion step uses multi-head cross-attention \citep{vaswani2017attention}, treating the current trajectory as query and retrieved cases as keys and values. Formally, given the query embedding $\mathbf{q} = \text{BiGRU}(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ of the target battery and the $k$ retrieved memory embeddings $\mathbf{M} = [\mathbf{m}_1; \ldots; \mathbf{m}_k] \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ with associated health labels $\mathbf{V}_{mem} = [\text{SOH}_1; \ldots; \text{SOH}_k]$, the cross-attention fusion computes:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{h}} = \text{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{q} + \text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{q},\;\mathbf{M},\;\mathbf{M})\right)
\label{eq:hero_cross_attn}
\end{equation}

where each of the $h{=}8$ heads projects $\mathbf{q}$ and $\mathbf{M}$ into separate subspaces (Eqs.~\ref{eq:multihead_individual}--\ref{eq:multihead_concat}), enabling the model to simultaneously attend to different aspects of similarity---for example, one head may focus on voltage-plateau shape while another captures temperature-history alignment. The residual connection preserves the target battery's identity, and the layer normalisation stabilises gradients across chemistries. The fused representation $\hat{\mathbf{h}}$ is then passed through a feed-forward block and split into separate SOH and RUL prediction heads. Thus, the proven architecture from NLP literature is extended to battery data, where each attention head learns a distinct degradation-relevant similarity metric.




\subsubsection{Detection and Diagnosis}

Accurate state-of-health prediction is necessary but not sufficient for effective battery management. Users need advance notice when degradation accelerates beyond expected levels, providing time for maintenance planning or operational adjustments before critical capacity loss occurs. Conventional battery management systems trigger warnings when SOH crosses a fixed threshold, typically 80--85\%. While simple to implement, this approach provides limited lead time and fails to detect rapid degradation events until significant capacity has already been lost.

We developed a slope-based early warning system that monitors the rate of capacity decline rather than absolute SOH values, enabling earlier detection of accelerating degradation. The key insight is that degradation rate changes contain more actionable information than absolute capacity values. A battery declining at 0.05\% per cycle follows a gradual, predictable trajectory. The same battery suddenly losing 0.25\% per cycle signals an acute problem that requires immediate attention, even if absolute SOH remains above 85\%.

The algorithm computes a rolling linear regression over a 20-cycle window to estimate the current degradation rate:
\begin{equation}
\text{slope} = \frac{d(\text{SOH})}{d(\text{cycle})} \approx \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{20} (c_i - \bar{c})(s_i - \bar{s})}{\sum_{i=1}^{20} (c_i - \bar{c})^2}
\end{equation}
where $c_i$ represents cycle number, $s_i$ represents SOH at that cycle, and $\bar{c}$, $\bar{s}$ denote mean values over the window. A warning triggers when the degradation rate exceeds 0.17\% SOH loss per cycle, a threshold empirically optimized on NASA Ames battery data from 34 cells to balance early detection against false alarm rate. This threshold reflects the observation that gradual aging typically proceeds at 0.05--0.10\% per cycle, while accelerated degradation from lithium plating, mechanical damage, or thermal stress manifests as 0.20--0.40\% per cycle.

The early warning system serves as a diagnostic trigger for deeper investigation. When degradation rate exceeds the threshold, the system automatically invokes causal mechanism attribution to identify which electrochemical process is responsible for the acceleration. This integration enables not just early detection, but diagnoses the root cause (i.e., a specific mechanism such as lithium plating from cold-temperature charging, etc.) if the battery is degrading faster than expected. The subsequent counterfactual intervention framework then recommends specific corrective actions based on the identified mechanism.



\subsection{Physics-Informed Causal Attribution}

Once we know how much capacity has degraded and whether degradation is accelerating, the next step is to evaluate the reasons for the same. For this purpose, the Hybrid Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) is used. Unlike the previous components that treat degradation as a single number, the PINN decomposes observed capacity loss into contributions from five specific electrochemical mechanisms: SEI layer growth, lithium plating, active material loss, electrolyte decomposition, and current collector corrosion.
Predicting that a battery will lose capacity is useful information. Knowing that it is losing capacity because of lithium plating during cold-weather fast-charging provides an actionable intervention. This is the key difference the developed framework provides compared to state-of-the-art BMS systems.

\paragraph{Multi-head attention for mechanism attribution.}
The attribution architecture mirrors multi-head attention: a shared encoder produces a fused representation that is then processed by $M{=}5$ independent mechanism heads, each specialising in a single degradation mode. Given input features $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_f}$ and operating context $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}$, the shared encoder computes:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h} = \text{Fusion}\!\left(\text{Enc}_f(\mathbf{x})\;\|\;\text{Enc}_c(\mathbf{c})\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}
\label{eq:pinn_shared_enc}
\end{equation}

where $\|$ denotes concatenation, $\text{Enc}_f$ and $\text{Enc}_c$ are two-layer encoders with LayerNorm and GELU activations, and Fusion is a linear projection followed by LayerNorm. Each mechanism head $m \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$ then independently scores the fused representation:

\begin{equation}
s_m = \text{Head}_m(\mathbf{h}) = \mathbf{W}_m^{(2)} \,\sigma\!\left(\mathbf{W}_m^{(1)} \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b}_m^{(1)}\right) + b_m^{(2)}
\label{eq:mechanism_heads}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}_m^{(1)}, \mathbf{W}_m^{(2)}$ are head-specific weight matrices and $\sigma$ is the GELU activation. This design is analogous to multi-head attention (Eq.~\ref{eq:multihead_individual}): each head attends to the same shared representation but learns a distinct projection---here, each head specialises in detecting the signature of one electrochemical mechanism rather than attending to different positions in a sequence.

The total capacity loss is constrained to equal the sum of individual mechanism attributions:
\begin{equation}
Q_{loss} = \alpha_{SEI} + \alpha_{plating} + \alpha_{AM} + \alpha_{electrolyte} + \alpha_{corrosion}
\end{equation}

The training objective combines prediction accuracy with physics constraints:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{SOH} + \lambda_1 \left| \sum \alpha_i - (1 - SOH) \right| + \lambda_2 \mathcal{L}_{prior}
\end{equation}

The first term, $\mathcal{L}_{SOH}$, is the standard mean squared error between predicted and observed SOH. The middle term enforces mass conservation. Since each $\alpha_i$ represents the fractional capacity loss attributed to mechanism $i$, their sum must equal the total observed capacity degradation $(1 - SOH)$. The absolute value penalizes deviations from this physical requirement, preventing the model from ``creating'' or ``losing'' capacity through mathematical artifacts. We set $\lambda_1 = 0.5$ to strongly enforce this constraint while still allowing the model flexibility to learn mechanism patterns as empirical tuning showed that higher values ($\lambda_1 > 0.7$) caused training instability, while lower values ($\lambda_1 < 0.3$) led to unphysical attributions. The third term, $\mathcal{L}_{prior}$, incorporates domain knowledge through physics-based priors, weighted by $\lambda_2 = 0.3$ to guide the model toward electrochemically plausible attributions without overriding data-driven learning. This specific value was chosen because empirical tuning demonstrated that 0.3 provides the optimal balance between strictly enforcing physical rules and allowing the neural network sufficient flexibility for data-driven discovery.

A central challenge in battery degradation modeling is distinguishing between competing electrochemical mechanisms that produce similar observable signatures. Consider a battery operating at 25°C with moderate charge and discharge rates. In this case, both SEI growth and active material loss are plausible explanations for the observed capacity fade. Standard machine learning approaches struggle to disambiguate such cases. We investigated three distinct architectures, each representing a different philosophy for integrating physics knowledge into neural network learning. The Pure PINN approach attempts to learn mechanism attribution entirely from electrochemical equations, without explicit domain rules. The architecture consists of a shared encoder that processes input features, followed by mechanism-specific physics networks that predict parameters for each degradation equation.

Each degradation mechanism is represented by an analytical electrochemical equation (e.g., Arrhenius kinetics for SEI growth, Butler-Volmer kinetics for lithium plating) whose unknown rate constants and exponents are treated as \emph{learnable parameters} optimised by backpropagation subject to hard physical bounds from the electrochemistry literature (e.g., the SEI activation energy $E_a$ is constrained to 35--60~kJ/mol). In the Hybrid PINN, the physics equations generate \emph{prior logits} $\mathbf{z}_{prior}$ that are added to the neural-network logits $\mathbf{z}_{NN}$ before the final softmax (Eq.~\ref{eq:hybrid_combination}), analogous to an additive bias in an attention score: just as position embeddings bias attention toward nearby tokens, the physics priors bias the attribution toward electrochemically plausible mechanisms under the observed operating conditions.

For SEI layer growth, we embed the established Arrhenius-diffusion model directly into the network:
\begin{equation}
Q_{SEI} = k_{SEI} \sqrt{t} \exp\left(-\frac{E_a}{2RT}\right) f(SOC)
\label{eq:sei_physics}
\end{equation}
where the activation energy $E_a$ (constrained to 35--60 kJ/mol to match literature values \citep{broussely2005main}) and rate constant $k_{SEI}$ are learned parameters, while $t$ represents time, $R$ is the gas constant, and $T$ is absolute temperature. The SOC-dependent factor $f(SOC) = 1 + 0.5(SOC - 0.5)^2$ captures the known acceleration of SEI growth at high state of charge.

For lithium plating, we implement a simplified Butler-Volmer kinetic model:
\begin{equation}
Q_{plating} = k_p \cdot \sigma\left(\frac{T_{crit} - T}{\delta_T}\right) \cdot \exp\left(\frac{T_{ref} - T}{\tau_T}\right) \cdot C_{rate}^\alpha
\label{eq:plating_physics}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ denotes the sigmoid function, $T_{crit} = 278.15$ K represents the critical temperature below which plating becomes significant, and $\alpha$ is a learned intercalation coefficient (constrained to 0.3--0.7). The temperature-dependent terms capture the well-established phenomenon that plating risk increases dramatically in cold conditions \citep{waldmann2014temperature}.

For active material loss due to mechanical stress, we adopt a power-law fatigue model:
\begin{equation}
Q_{AM} = k_{AM} \cdot C_{rate}^\beta \cdot N_{cycles}^\gamma
\label{eq:am_physics}
\end{equation}
where the stress exponent $\beta$ (constrained to 1.0--2.0) and cycle exponent $\gamma$ (constrained to 0.3--1.0) are learned from data. These bounds reflect established fatigue mechanics where capacity loss scales super-linearly with mechanical stress \citep{zhang2020degradation}. The stress exponent $\beta$ captures how aggressively discharge current-driven volume changes cause particle fracture: values near 1.0 indicate linear scaling (mild stress accumulation), while values approaching 2.0 reflect accelerated crack propagation from high-strain cycling. The cycle exponent $\gamma$ represents cumulative fatigue damage—values below 0.5 suggest rapid early-life damage that plateaus, while values near 1.0 indicate steady accumulation throughout lifetime. The learned value of $\beta = 1.48$ (reported in Section 4.9.2) suggests that the datasets contain primarily moderate-to-high stress cycling, consistent with the inclusion of XJTU 2C data.

For electrolyte decomposition, we model oxidative decomposition at the cathode surface using first-order kinetics with voltage-dependent activation:
\begin{equation}
Q_{elec} = k_{elec} \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{E_{a,elec}}{RT}\right) \cdot \left(1 + \kappa \cdot \max(0, V - V_{threshold})\right) \cdot t_{cycle}
\label{eq:electrolyte_physics}
\end{equation}
where $k_{elec}$ is a learned rate constant, $E_{a,elec}$ (constrained to 40--80 kJ/mol) is the activation energy for oxidation reactions \citep{vetter2005ageing}, $V$ is the operating voltage, $V_{threshold} = 4.1$ V represents the onset of significant decomposition, $\kappa$ is a voltage sensitivity parameter, and $t_{cycle}$ is cumulative cycling time. The voltage-dependent term captures the known acceleration of electrolyte oxidation at high voltages (>4.2 V), particularly relevant for high-nickel cathodes.

For current collector corrosion, we implement a simplified Tafel-based dissolution model:
\begin{equation}
Q_{corr} = k_{corr} \cdot \sigma\left(\frac{SOC_{threshold} - SOC}{\delta_{SOC}}\right) \cdot t_{storage} \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{E_{a,corr}}{RT}\right)
\label{eq:corrosion_physics}
\end{equation}
where $k_{corr}$ is a learned rate constant, $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, $SOC_{threshold} = 0.25$ (25\%) represents the state of charge below which copper dissolution becomes thermodynamically favorable \citep{schmalstieg2014electrochemical}, $\delta_{SOC}$ controls the transition sharpness, $t_{storage}$ is cumulative storage time, and $E_{a,corr}$ (constrained to 30--50 kJ/mol) is the activation energy for copper dissolution. The SOC-dependent term reflects that low-voltage storage (below ~3.0 V for typical lithium-ion cells) drives copper oxidation at the anode current collector.

The Pure PINN loss function combines classification accuracy with physics residual terms:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{Pure} = \mathcal{L}_{CE}(\hat{y}, y) + \lambda_{res} \sum_{m} \left\| Q_m^{pred} - Q_m^{obs} \right\|^2 + \lambda_{reg} \mathcal{R}(\theta_{phys})
\label{eq:pure_pinn_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{CE}$ is the cross-entropy classification loss, the residual term penalizes discrepancies between physics-predicted and observed capacity loss for each mechanism $m$, and $\mathcal{R}(\theta_{phys})$ regularizes learned physics parameters toward literature values.

Training the Pure PINN on 11,066 samples from NASA, XJTU, CALCE, and Oxford datasets yielded encouraging results for physics parameter learning. The network recovered activation energy $E_a = 35$ kJ/mol (falls within the range of 35--60 kJ/mol given in the literature), stress exponent $\beta = 1.48$ (matches closely to the literature value of approximately 1.5), and cycle exponent $\gamma = 0.52$ (falls within the range 0.3--1.0 given in the literature). However, mechanism classification accuracy reached only 60.0\% when evaluated on 75 benchmark scenarios spanning diverse operating conditions.


Analysis of the Pure PINN's failure cases revealed a fundamental limitation. At moderate operating conditions (temperatures between 20--40°C, charge rates between 0.5--1.5C), both SEI growth and active material loss equations produce similar capacity loss predictions. Figure~\ref{fig:pinn_boundary} illustrates this overlap region.

The physics equations in Eq.~\ref{eq:sei_physics} and Eq.~\ref{eq:am_physics} do not contain sufficient discriminative information because both mechanisms are genuinely active under moderate conditions. The relative contribution depends on factors not captured in the equations themselves, such as electrode microstructure, electrolyte formulation, and cell manufacturing variability. These factors are known to electrochemists but are difficult to parameterize from voltage-current measurements alone.

We tested whether additional training data at the SEI/active material loss boundary could address this limitation. Training with 5,000 boundary samples improved accuracy to 70.7\%; with 10,000 samples, accuracy reached 77.3\%; but with 20,000 samples, accuracy actually decreased to 73.3\% due to overfitting. This experiment demonstrates that the accuracy ceiling is not a data limitation but a fundamental physics limitation. The governing equations themselves do not uniquely specify mechanism identity in the overlapping region.

To overcome the disambiguation ceiling, we developed a Hybrid PINN architecture that combines three complementary learning components:

\begin{enumerate}[(\alph*)]
    \item {Neural Network Residual Learning:} A multi-layer encoder-decoder learns data-driven patterns from features and context, producing per-mechanism logits $\mathbf{z}_{NN} \in \mathbb{R}^5$.
    
    \item {Physics-Constrained Parameter Networks:} Mechanism-specific networks predict physics parameters (activation energies, stress exponents) with enforced physical bounds, enabling the physics equations to inform prediction.
    
    \item {Expert Domain Priors:} Rule-based priors encode electrochemical knowledge about mechanism-operating condition relationships, producing prior logits $\mathbf{z}_{prior} \in \mathbb{R}^5$.
\end{enumerate}

The final prediction combines the above components and represented by the equation given below.
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{z}_{prior} + \mathbf{z}_{NN})
\label{eq:hybrid_combination}
\end{equation}
The expert priors encode domain knowledge as follows. The \emph{SEI Growth Prior} assigns elevated probability during storage mode or gentle cycling (charge rate $< 1.0$C and discharge rate $< 1.0$C), with temperature-dependent scaling following Arrhenius kinetics to capture the thermally activated nature of side reactions. The \emph{Lithium Plating Prior} strongly elevates probability when temperature falls below 10°C during cycling with active charging this threshold is stricter than some literature values (which suggest 15--17°C) to minimize false positives in borderline cases where intercalation kinetics merely slow but do not fully fail. The \emph{Active Material Loss Prior} elevates probability during cycling with high discharge rates ($> 1.5$C) or combined high charge and discharge rates, while suppressing it during moderate operation. This reflects the mechanical stress paradigm where rapid lithium insertion/extraction drives volume changes that fracture active material particles. The \emph{Electrolyte Decomposition Prior} increases probability at elevated temperatures ($> 35$°C) and high voltages ($> 4.1$ V). Unlike the kinetic threshold priors, this prior is mathematically formulated directly from oxidation thermodynamics at the cathode-electrolyte interface, computing a continuous loss penalty based on cumulative high-voltage exposure. Finally, the \emph{Collector Corrosion Prior} is strongly elevated only during storage at very low state of charge (SOC $< 25\%$), where copper dissolution becomes thermodynamically favorable due to the reducing environment at low anode potentials.

These priors are not arbitrary heuristics but distillations of established electrochemical knowledge \citep{vetter2005ageing, waldmann2014temperature, zhang2020degradation}. Critically, they address precisely the disambiguation cases where pure physics fails. For example, at moderate temperatures and C-rates, the priors encode the domain-expert judgment that SEI typically dominates unless C-rates are sufficiently high to cause mechanical stress.


The Hybrid PINN architecture contains 114,252 trainable parameters, distributed across the feature encoder (17,664), context encoder (4,288), fusion layer (24,832), physics parameter networks (8,135), and mechanism heads (33,285). The physics equations and expert priors add zero trainable parameters as they are embedded as structured inductive biases.

The final classification and attribution are driven by a composite loss function:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{Hybrid} = \mathcal{L}_{SOH} + \lambda_1 \mathcal{L}_{mass} + \lambda_2 \mathcal{L}_{prior}
\label{eq:hybrid_loss}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Counterfactual Optimization}

The counterfactual optimization module is not a separately trained model. It is an inference-time technique that reuses the already-trained Hybrid PINN (Stage 3) as an oracle. Given a proposed change to operating conditions, it constructs a modified input feature vector, performs a single forward pass through the Hybrid PINN to obtain the counterfactual attribution, and compares the result against the current attribution. No additional training data, loss functions, or gradient computation are required.

The causal attribution framework answers one critical question: which electrochemical mechanism is responsible for observed degradation? But from an operational standpoint, a second question is equally important: what should we do about it? Knowing that lithium plating accounts for 70\% of capacity fade is valuable diagnostic information, but the user still needs actionable guidance on how to mitigate the problem right now. This is where most battery management systems stop they can detect degradation and even attribute it to specific mechanisms, but cannot recommend immediate corrective actions. We address this gap through a novel framework based on causal counterfactual reasoning \citep{pearl2009causality,peters2017elements}.

\paragraph{Illustrative example of counterfactual what-if reasoning.}
To give reviewers an intuitive grasp of the mechanism before the formal treatment, consider a single concrete battery from the NASA Ames dataset (cell B0005) being fast-charged at 0\textdegree{}C with 1.5C current. The Hybrid PINN attribution network first scores the current operating conditions and returns a diagnosis: lithium plating dominates at 70\%, with SEI growth at 20\% and active material loss at 10\%. The counterfactual module then constructs three hypothetical ``what-if'' scenarios by modifying one or two input features at a time while holding everything else fixed, and passes each modified feature vector through the same trained PINN to obtain a new attribution. Scenario~A (reduce charging current from 3.0~A to 1.5~A): the lower current reduces anode overpotential below the lithium deposition threshold, and the model predicts plating drops from 70\% to 10\%. Scenario~B (warm the battery from 0\textdegree{}C to 15\textdegree{}C): higher temperature accelerates lithium-ion diffusion into graphite, and predicted plating falls to 5\%. Scenario~C (apply both interventions simultaneously): plating is completely eliminated (70\%~$\rightarrow$~0\%), with SEI growth temporarily rising to 40\% as the dominant---but slower and more manageable---mechanism. The optimizer ranks Scenario~C highest and issues the recommendation: ``Preheat battery to 15\textdegree{}C and reduce charging current to 1.5~A.'' This entire evaluation over 10--15 candidates takes less than one second on a standard CPU, making the approach feasible for real-time BMS deployment.

The formal treatment generalises this example. Traditional approaches rely on hand-crafted rules or lookup tables derived from offline optimisation. These methods lack the ability to reason about novel scenarios and do not leverage the causal structure already learned by the Hybrid PINN. We take a fundamentally different approach: we use the trained model to simulate hypothetical scenarios and select the intervention that yields the most favourable mechanism redistribution.

Formally, let $\mathbf{s}$ represent the current battery state (SOC, temperature, current, voltage, cycle count) and $\alpha(\mathbf{s})$ denote the current mechanism attribution vector. Given a proposed intervention $\mathbf{i}$, we define the counterfactual state $\mathbf{s}' = \text{apply}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{i})$ and predict the counterfactual attribution $\alpha'(\mathbf{s}')$ using the same trained model. The $\text{apply}(\cdot)$ operation performs targeted feature substitution modifying only the intervention-targeted feature while holding all other state variables fixed. This is a first-order approximation. In practice, coupled effects exist (e.g., reducing current slightly lowers Joule heating), but the Hybrid PINN partially compensates through its learned physics priors. The intervention effect is quantified as:
\begin{equation}
\Delta(\mathbf{i}) = \sum_{m} w_m \left[ \alpha_m(\mathbf{s}) - \alpha_m'(\mathbf{s}') \right]
\end{equation}
where $m$ indexes the five degradation mechanisms and $w_m$ represents mechanism-specific priority weights. We assign higher weights to irreversible mechanisms (plating, active material loss: $w = 2.0$) and lower weights to partially reversible ones (SEI growth: $w = 1.5$, electrolyte loss: $w = 0.8$), reflecting the relative permanence of each failure mode.

Given a set of candidate interventions $\mathcal{I}$, the optimization problem is:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{i}^* = \argmax_{\mathbf{i} \in \mathcal{I}} \Delta(\mathbf{i})
\end{equation}
subject to practical feasibility constraints (temperature limits: 15--45°C, current limits: 0.1--2.5A, SOC constraints). Candidates are generated systematically based on the dominant mechanism and established electrochemical thresholds. For \emph{lithium plating}, current reductions of $-$0.5A to $-$1.5A target the anode overpotential below the deposition threshold ($\eta < 0$ V vs. Li/Li$^+$) \citep{petzl2015lithium}, and temperature increases of $+$5 to $+$15°C exploit Arrhenius-governed diffusivity improvements \citep{waldmann2014temperature}. For \emph{SEI growth}, SOC targets are lowered to 80--90\% to keep the anode above 120~mV vs. Li/Li$^+$ \citep{pinson2013theory}, and temperature is reduced by 5--10°C to slow kinetics ($E_a \approx 0.4$ eV \citep{broussely2005main}). For \emph{active material loss}, C-rate is capped at 1C, above which diffusion-induced stress scales as $C^{1.5}$ and exceeds fracture toughness \citep{birkl2017degradation}. For \emph{electrolyte decomposition} and \emph{corrosion}, interventions avoid high-SOC exposure ($> 90\%$) and over-discharge conditions respectively.

For each candidate, the Hybrid PINN simulates the counterfactual attribution and ranks interventions by $\Delta(\mathbf{i})$. A typical optimization over 10--15 candidates takes less than 1 second on a standard CPU, making it suitable for real-time deployment. Each recommendation is accompanied by an interpretable breakdown showing how each mechanism would change under the proposed intervention, along with relevant trade-offs (e.g., charging time increase from current reduction). This transparency builds user trust as the operator understands not just what to do, but why it works.

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first application of causal counterfactual reasoning to battery health management. Prior work in battery optimization has relied on model predictive control \citep{torchio2016lionsimba} or rule-based expert systems. Our approach differs fundamentally in four key aspects. First, \emph{causal grounding} ensures that interventions are designed based on causal mechanisms rather than mere empirical correlations, providing a principled foundation for recommendations. Second, \emph{interpretability} is built-in, as each recommendation comes with a mechanistic explanation showing why the intervention works and how it affects each degradation pathway. Third, \emph{computational efficiency} is achieved because no online optimization is required—the system leverages the pre-trained causal model for counterfactual simulation, enabling real-time response. Fourth, \emph{generalization} across diverse chemistries and operating conditions is possible because the causal mechanisms transfer between battery types, unlike black-box models that require retraining for each new chemistry.

The counterfactual framework bridges diagnosis and action, completing the loop from mechanism attribution to operational intervention. It transforms the battery management system from a passive monitor into an active advisor that can guide users toward degradation-minimizing strategies.



\subsection{User Advisory System}



When the attribution network identifies SEI growth as the primary degradation mechanism, the system transitions to storage-mode advisory. However, before issuing storage-specific recommendations, we must first determine whether the battery is genuinely in a storage scenario or simply undergoing low-intensity cycling. This distinction is critical as providing cycling advice during calendar aging would be ineffective at best and misleading at worst.

To address this challenge, we introduce a Physics-Aware Temporal Transformer (PATT) for robust domain classification. While conventional data-driven models (e.g., Multilayer Perceptrons) can classify operating modes based on instantaneous features, they lack the inductive bias required to distinguish between slow calendar aging and gentle cycling, often leading to misclassification in ambiguous edge cases.

PATT overcomes this limitation by embedding electrochemical principles directly into the neural architecture. Instead of standard positional encodings, the model utilizes learnable physics-informed embeddings:
\begin{enumerate}[(a)]
    \item Arrhenius Embedding: Captures temperature-dependent degradation kinetics ($\exp(-E_a/k_B T)$), critical for scaling aging rates across diverse thermal environments.
    \item Diffusion Embedding: Incorporates a square-root time term ($\sqrt{t}$), reflecting transport-limited mechanisms such as Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) growth which dominate calendar aging.
\end{enumerate}

These physics priors are processed by a multi-head self-attention mechanism that learns to weigh the thermodynamic (storage) and kinetic (cycling) drivers of degradation. We trained and validated PATT on a comprehensive dataset combining the Stanford Calendar Aging dataset (60 cells, spanning up to 69 months) with a diverse cycling aggregation (73 cells from NASA, CALCE, Oxford, and XJTU, covering various C-rates and usage profiles).

The advisory system's decision logic follows a two-stage process. First, PATT classifies the current usage context (storage or cycling). This classification is then used by the advisory layer to select the appropriate pool of candidate counterfactual interventions: for a storage context, the optimizer evaluates SOC reduction and temperature control; for a cycling context, it evaluates C-rate and preheating strategies. The final recommendation is the highest-ranked intervention from the contextually appropriate pool, accompanied by a quantified impact estimate.




Once storage mode is confirmed, the practical implications become significant \citep{schimpe2018comprehensive, ecker2014calendar}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Storing at 100\% SOC, 30°C: 8.2\% capacity loss per year
    \item Storing at 60\% SOC, 30°C: 2.1\% capacity loss per year
\end{itemize}

We further validated this approach on 475 EIS spectra from 94 cells in the Stanford Calendar Aging dataset, spanning temperatures from $-$40°C to 50°C and storage periods ranging from 3 weeks to 6 months (see Table~\ref{tab:datasets}). The classifier maintained robust performance across these diverse conditions, including challenging edge cases like extreme cold environments that many datasets exclude.




When conditions favor lithium plating (temperature below 5°C combined with fast charging), the system generates warnings that lithium plating risk is high and recommends preheating the battery to 15°C before fast charging, or reducing the charge rate to 0.5C.

\subsection{Model Training Details}

Before presenting the validation results on unseen data, we first summarise the training dynamics of all three learned components of our framework: HERO, the Hybrid PINN, and PATT.

\subsubsection{HERO Training Dynamics}

The bidirectional GRU encoder and cross-attention fusion head were jointly trained using the AdamW optimizer (learning rate 0.001, batch size 64) on 3,979 degradation trajectories from five datasets. The MSE loss drops rapidly in the first 30 epochs as the GRU learns compact trajectory fingerprints, then converges smoothly below 0.10. Validation and training curves track closely throughout, confirming that the retrieval-augmented architecture does not overfit to the training chemistries (see Figure~S1 in the Supplementary Material for full training curves). The final held-out test SOH MAE of 0.74\% (R\textsuperscript{2} = 0.990) is reached without any fine-tuning on the target chemistry, supporting the zero-shot transfer claim.

\subsubsection{Hybrid PINN Training Dynamics}

The Hybrid PINN successfully learned meaningful representations during training. The complete training trajectory over 300 epochs and early-phase dynamics are provided in Figures~S2 and S3 of the Supplementary Material.


The training dynamics reveal several important insights. First, the rapid convergence to 92.0\% validation accuracy within the first few epochs demonstrates that the physics-informed architecture provides strong inductive biases that guide learning toward physically meaningful solutions. Second, the stability of validation accuracy throughout training, without degradation or significant fluctuation, confirms that the model generalizes well to held-out scenarios rather than memorizing training examples. Third, the decomposition of total loss (as detailed in Section 4.3) shows that the mass conservation penalty contributed approximately 30\% of the final loss and the expert prior loss contributed 15\%, with the remaining 55\% from SOH prediction error. This balance indicates that the model successfully integrates physics constraints and domain knowledge while maintaining predictive accuracy. Note that the 92.0\% figure reflects the validation accuracy measured on augmented training splits during convergence monitoring, while the final 96.0\% test accuracy reported throughout this paper is measured on the unaugmented, held-out 75 benchmark scenarios. The higher test accuracy occurs because augmented training variants introduce slight distributional perturbations that moderately challenge the validation monitor, whereas the held-out scenarios span cleaner, canonical operating conditions.
Having established that the model converged to a stable solution that respects physical principles, we now evaluate its performance on completely unseen battery datasets and operating conditions.

The advisory system requires a second learned component: the Physics-Aware Temporal Transformer (PATT) that classifies whether the battery is currently in storage mode or cycling mode. This distinction is critical because recommended actions differ dramatically between these domains (see Figure~S4 in the Supplementary Material for PATT training dynamics).

The PATT model contains 69,862 parameters and was trained on 4,936 samples from 133 real battery cells: 73 cycling cells (NASA 34, CALCE 5, Oxford 8, XJTU 26) and 60 storage cells from the Stanford Calendar Aging dataset (up to 69 months). The rapid convergence and stable validation accuracy (99.7\% during training, 99.2\% on held-out test) demonstrate that physics-informed embeddings provide strong inductive biases for domain classification. The learned physics parameters ($\alpha_{Arrhenius} = 0.50$, $\beta_{\sqrt{t}} = 0.30$) converged to non-zero stable values, confirming that the model actively exploits thermodynamic (temperature-dependent SEI growth) and kinetic (diffusion-limited aging) principles rather than treating them as dead parameters. This physics-guided learning explains why PATT outperforms the purely data-driven MLP baseline (99.2\% vs 92.2\%), particularly in ambiguous cases like gentle cycling versus extended storage at moderate SOC.

\subsubsection{Zero-Shot Generalization via Lithium Features}

 Standard models fail when applied to chemistries not seen during training. We address this through lithium inventory features: eleven physics-based variables that act as chemistry-invariant fingerprints. These include: differential voltage analysis (dV/dQ slope) correlating with phase transitions \citep{bloom2005differential}; voltage plateau characteristics (center potential and width) indicating available lithium inventory \citep{dubarry2017synthesize}; capacity fade rate (dQ/dN) and resistance growth rate (dR/dN) \citep{vetter2005ageing}; lithium utilization factor, defined as the ratio of cyclable lithium to host capacity \citep{birkl2017degradation}; a plating risk index derived from overpotential at the anode interface \citep{waldmann2014temperature}; entropy coefficient $dU/dT$ characterizing the thermodynamic state via temperature dependence of OCV; relaxation time constants indicating transport limitations through voltage recovery scales; coulombic efficiency trends tracking irreversible side reactions; incremental resistance $dV/dI$ from high-frequency response; an effective diffusivity proxy derived from voltage response during constant-current phases \citep{ecker2015parameterization}; and a calendar aging factor reflecting SOC-dependent SEI growth rate during rest periods \citep{keil2016calendar}.

The comparison deserves careful explanation because the setup differs fundamentally from typical benchmarks. We did not split a single dataset into train and test sets that would test memorization, not generalization. Instead, we trained all models on LCO (Lithium Cobalt Oxide) chemistry batteries (NASA, CALCE, Oxford datasets: 4,000 samples total) and evaluated on completely unseen NCM/NCA chemistry (TJU dataset). This is a true zero-shot transfer scenario, as it involves new cathode material, different voltage curves, and altered degradation kinetics.

To demonstrate that HERO's performance gains stem from retrieval-augmented learning combined with lithium inventory features rather than simply using a more complex architecture, we benchmarked it against standard machine learning approaches. These baselines represent the current state-of-the-art in battery prognostics: tree-based methods (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) that excel at tabular data, and deep learning architectures (MLP, LSTM, GRU, Transformer, CNN-LSTM) commonly used for time-series prediction. The baseline models saw this chemistry for the first time during testing and thus no fine-tuning, or adaptation was involved, and just direct inference is drawn. For Random Forest and Gradient Boosting, we trained separate regressors for SOH and RUL. For neural networks (MLP, LSTM, GRU, Transformer, CNN-LSTM), we built standard architectures: two hidden layers of 128/64 units for MLP, 64-unit hidden states for recurrent models, 4-head attention for Transformers. We used the same features for everyone: voltage, current, temperature, SOC, cycle count, and basic dV/dQ derivatives computed from charge curves. The playing field was level and only the architecture varied.

Training followed identical protocols: AdamW optimizer, learning rate of 0.001, 200 epochs, MSE loss for regression targets. We scaled features using StandardScaler fitted on training data and applied to test data. Batch size was 64 for all neural models. For reproducibility, HERO uses a bidirectional GRU encoder (2 layers, 256 hidden units, 128-dimensional latent space), cosine similarity retrieval from a 3,979-trajectory memory bank ($k{=}5$ for standard inference, $k{=}3$ for zero-shot transfer, with a $\pm$5\% SOH filter relaxed to 10\% if insufficient matches are found), 8-head cross-attention fusion, and is trained with AdamW (learning rate 0.001, batch size 64) for 200 epochs using MSE loss.

To compare the results from different models, we ran each model once with the same random seed (42 for training, 123 for test set generation). The negative R\textsuperscript{2} values from baseline methods were initially surprising, but they make physical sense: when battery chemistry changes, learned patterns break. A model trained to recognize LCO's voltage plateau around 3.9V will misinterpret NCA's 3.7V characteristic. Without chemistry-invariant features the lithium inventory calculations and retrieval-augmented context that HERO uses pure pattern matching fails.

HERO distinguishes itself not through raw accuracy but through capability. It is the only method providing  zero-shot generalization to unseen chemistries while simultaneously offering interpretable causal attribution. Other approaches require complete retraining when chemistry changes whereas HERO adapts through its memory bank and physics-based features.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty Quantification}

To provide statistically rigorous performance bounds, we computed bootstrap confidence intervals (1,000 resamples, 95\% confidence) on a held-out test set of 274 samples spanning all five datasets. Bootstrap resampling creates multiple pseudo-datasets by sampling with replacement from the original test set, enabling robust estimation of metric variability without parametric assumptions. The 95\% confidence intervals (CI) represent the range within which we expect the true population metric to fall with 95\% probability.

For State of Health (SOH) Prediction, HERO achieves a mean absolute error (MAE) of 0.69\% for SOH estimation, with a 95\% confidence interval of [0.63\%, 0.75\%]. This means that on average, HERO's SOH predictions deviate from true values by less than 1\%, and we can be 95\% confident that the true error lies between 0.63\% and 0.75\%. For context, a battery with 85\% SOH would be predicted within $\pm$0.69\%, or roughly $\pm$0.6 absolute percentage points. The coefficient of determination (R\textsuperscript{2}) of 0.990 (95\% CI: [0.988, 0.992]) indicates that HERO explains 99\% of the variance in SOH degradation trajectories. The narrow confidence interval width of 0.12\% (0.75\% - 0.63\%) demonstrates that this performance is consistent across different subsets of the test data, not dependent on specific battery samples.

For RUL estimation, HERO achieves a MAE of 16.2 cycles with a 95\% confidence interval of [14.5, 17.9] cycles. This indicates that when predicting how many cycles remain before a battery reaches its end-of-life threshold (typically 80\% SOH), HERO's predictions are off by an average of 16 cycles. For a battery with 200 remaining cycles, this represents approximately 8\% relative error. The R\textsuperscript{2} value of 0.981 (95\% CI: [0.975, 0.986]) confirms that the model captures 98\% of RUL variance. The confidence interval width of 3.4 cycles (17.9 - 14.5) is narrow relative to typical battery lifetimes of 500-2000 cycles, indicating stable predictive performance.

The narrow confidence intervals across both SOH and RUL metrics demonstrate that HERO's performance is statistically stable and reproducible, not the result of overfitting to specific test samples. Per-prediction uncertainty quantification of $\pm$0.99\% SOH (calculated as 1.96 times the standard error from bootstrap distributions) enables deployment in safety-critical battery management systems where confidence bounds are required for decision-making. When HERO reports an SOH estimate of 87\%, operators can trust that the true value lies within [86.0\%, 88.0\%] with 95\% probability, enabling informed maintenance scheduling and warranty decisions.

The Hybrid PINN training process begins with 75 carefully constructed benchmark scenarios spanning five datasets (NASA, XJTU, CALCE, Oxford, and TJU). Each scenario represents a specific cell-condition combination from Table~\ref{tab:datasets}: 15 from NASA Ames (covering 4--43°C, 0.5--2C), 15 from TJU NCM/NCA (1C, 25°C), 15 from Nature MATR (1--8C fast charging), 15 from randomized high-temperature usage (40°C stress tests), and 15 from HUST LFP (various cycling protocols). The dominant degradation mechanism for each scenario was assigned using a two-step labeling process. First, condition-based assignment applied established electrochemical rules: cells cycled below 10°C at $\geq$1C were labeled lithium plating dominant; cells in prolonged storage or gentle cycling at elevated temperature were labeled SEI growth; cells at $\geq$2C were labeled active material loss; and cells stored at SOC $<$25\% were labeled current collector corrosion. Second, where available, we validated these assignments against published post-mortem and incremental capacity analysis (ICA/DVA) results from the original dataset publications \citep{birkl2017degradation, waldmann2014temperature}. For the 23 scenarios where post-mortem data was available, condition-based labels agreed with experimental evidence in 22 cases (96\%), with the single disagreement occurring in a moderate-temperature case where both SEI and active material loss were co-dominant. To improve model robustness and prevent overfitting, we apply data augmentation by perturbing temperature ($\pm$2°C), current ($\pm$0.1A), and SOC ($\pm$3\%) for each scenario, generating 30 augmented variants per original sample. This yields 2,250 training examples from the 75 base scenarios.

Training used the AdamW optimizer \citep{loshchilov2019decoupled} with learning rate 0.001, weight decay 0.01, and batch size 32, resulting in 71 batches per epoch. The total loss combines three components as defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:hybrid_loss}: SOH prediction loss $\mathcal{L}_{SOH}$, mass conservation penalty with $\lambda_1 = 0.5$, and expert prior loss $\mathcal{L}_{prior}$ with $\lambda_2 = 0.3$. We trained for 300 epochs with early stopping based on validation accuracy plateauing.

The training dynamics reveal interesting behavior. In the initial epochs (1-10), the model rapidly learned basic patterns, achieving 94.0\% training accuracy by epoch 4 as it discovered simple heuristics like ``low temperature + high current $\rightarrow$ plating.'' However, validation accuracy showed more gradual improvement, reaching 92.0\% by epoch 2 and stabilizing thereafter. The gap between training (94.5\% at epoch 20) and validation (92.0\%) accuracy reflects the model's appropriate generalization, avoiding overfitting to the augmented training data. Total loss decreased from 1.0544 at epoch 1 to 0.9593 by epoch 20, with the mass conservation penalty contributing approximately 30\% of the total loss and the prior loss contributing 15\%, while the remaining 55\% came from SOH prediction error. After epoch 100, both loss and accuracy plateaued, indicating convergence. The final model achieves 96.0\% attribution accuracy on the held-out test set of 75 benchmark scenarios, with perfect (100\%) detection of safety-critical mechanisms (lithium plating and current collector corrosion). We validated the final Hybrid PINN on 75 test cases spanning five independent datasets never seen during training. These are shown in Table~\ref{tab:attribution}. Even for such unseen cases, an average accuracy of 96.0\% is obtained.

To validate our causal attribution model's ability to detect mechanical stress-induced degradation, we evaluated it on the XJTU battery dataset \citep{wang2024pinn}; 26 NCM cells cycled at high C-rates (2C, 2.5C, and 3C) at room temperature. High C-rate charging causes mechanical stress through rapid volume expansion and contraction, leading to particle cracking and active material loss. This physics is well-established in electrochemistry literature \citep{birkl2017degradation}.

Active Material Loss was correctly identified as the dominant degradation mechanism in 100\% of cases (26/26 cells). Importantly, the attribution scales monotonically with C-rate: 49.2\% at 2C, 62.7\% at 2.5C, and 69.2\% at 3C (see Table~S1 in the Supplementary Material for the full breakdown). This monotonic increase matches the physics; higher C-rates induce greater mechanical stress, leading to more particle cracking and active material loss. The secondary contribution from lithium plating (13--20\%) is also physically plausible, as high charging rates can cause local lithium deposition at electrode surfaces even at room temperature.
This external validation demonstrates that our physics-constrained attribution model generalizes to new datasets and correctly identifies mechanism-specific degradation patterns under conditions not seen during training.

Table~S2 in the Supplementary Material summarizes the performance comparison across architectures. All models were trained on identical data and evaluated on the same 75 benchmark scenarios.
Several observations merit discussion. First, the Pure PINN achieves perfect accuracy on SEI (100\%) when trained with adequate representation, but fails completely on rare mechanisms (0\% for plating and corrosion) when training data lacks examples. This reflects a fundamental data-dependency that physics constraints alone cannot overcome.
Second, the Boundary-Aware PINN, which augments training with synthetic samples at the SEI/active material loss decision boundary, substantially improves rare-mechanism detection but remains stuck at 53\% for active material loss. The physics equations simply cannot distinguish moderate-rate mechanical degradation from accelerated SEI growth.
Third, the Hybrid PINN achieves 96.0\% overall accuracy with strong performance across all mechanisms. The 18.7 percentage point improvement over Boundary-Aware PINN (96.0\% versus 77.3\%) demonstrates the value of encoding expert domain knowledge as structured priors within the learning framework.


To isolate the contribution of each component, we performed systematic ablation experiments (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). The results reveal a knowledge distillation effect: the neural network internalizes most of the prior knowledge during training. When all priors are removed at inference time, the NN alone achieves 93.3\%, only 2.7 percentage points below the full model. This occurs because the Hybrid loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:hybrid_loss}) jointly optimizes $\theta_{NN}$ with the prior alignment term $\lambda_2 \mathcal{L}_{prior}$, meaning the learned weights encode the physics heuristics that were originally explicit in the priors. Conversely, the physics priors alone (with NN zeroed) achieve 81.3\%, providing a strong rule-based baseline. Removing any \textit{single} prior at inference time has no measurable effect (accuracy remains 96.0\%), because the NN has learned compensatory representations that cover each prior's individual contribution.

The practical implication is nuanced. The neural network contribution alone (93.3\% minus the 21.7\% random baseline) accounts for 71.6 percentage points, while the priors add a remaining 2.7 points at inference. However, this understates the priors' true contribution: without the prior loss term during \textit{training}, the NN would not have learned physics-consistent representations. The priors thus serve dual roles: (1) as a training-time regularizer that shapes the learned representation space, and (2) as an inference-time safety net for edge cases, specifically two cold-weather lithium plating scenarios (Panasonic $-20$°C) that the NN alone misclassifies. For safety-critical battery management, this safety net is essential since misattributing lithium plating as SEI growth could lead to continued fast charging under conditions that risk thermal runaway.

We validated the counterfactual intervention framework on four real degradation scenarios extracted from the NASA and XJTU datasets, representing diverse failure modes and operating conditions (Figure~\ref{fig:counterfactual_validation}).

The counterfactual optimizer was validated using 4 specific scenarios selected from the NASA Ames and XJTU battery datasets: (1) NASA B0005 cell operating at 0°C with 1.5C charging exhibiting 70\% lithium plating attribution, (2) NASA B0006 cell at 10°C with 1C charging showing 50\% plating, (3) XJTU cell cycling at 25°C with 2C rate (high C-rate) demonstrating 55\% active material loss, and (4) XJTU cell at 28°C under extreme 3C cycling with 65\% active material loss. These scenarios were selected to span the range of dominant mechanisms (plating vs. mechanical stress) and operating conditions (temperature: 0-28°C, C-rate: 1-3C). The Hybrid PINN model used to generate counterfactual predictions was trained on 11,066 samples from all five datasets (NASA, XJTU, CALCE, Oxford, TJU) but validated on these held-out test scenarios that were not seen during training.


For Low-Temperature Plating (0°C, 1.5C) data samples of NASA dataset, the battery exhibited 70\% lithium plating attribution with severe capacity fade. The counterfactual optimizer recommended reducing current to 1.5A, which our simulation predicted would completely eliminate plating (70\% $\rightarrow$ 0\%) while maintaining acceptable charging time. This aligns with established electrochemical knowledge that lithium plating is strongly current- and temperature-dependent \citep{petzl2015lithium}.

For Moderate-Temperature Plating (10°C, 1C) data samples of NASA dataset, with 50\% plating attribution, the system recommended reducing current to 0.5A to eliminate plating entirely. The alternative of warming to 25°C would also be effective but takes longer to implement in practice. The counterfactual framework correctly identified both options and ranked them by predicted impact.

For High C-Rate data samples of XJTU dataset, the 55\% active material loss attribution reflects mechanical stress from high-rate cycling \citep{birkl2017degradation}. The recommended intervention was reducing C-rate to 1.25C (2.5A), predicted to achieve 26\% reduction in active material degradation. This is a more modest improvement compared to the plating scenarios because mechanical degradation depends on cumulative stress history, not just instantaneous conditions.

For Very High C-Rate (3C, 28°C) data samples of XJTU dataset, with 65\% active material loss under extreme conditions, the framework recommended reducing to 2.25A, predicting 4\% mechanism reduction. The limited improvement reflects physical constraints; at such high C-rates, even moderate reduction does not fully eliminate mechanical stress. This demonstrates that the counterfactual framework provides realistic predictions rather than over-promising benefits.

Across all four scenarios, the average dominant mechanism reduction was 34.6\%, with perfect elimination (100\% reduction) achieved in low-temperature plating cases and modest but meaningful reductions in high C-rate scenarios. To quantify alignment with known optimal strategies, we adopted three criteria: (1) the recommended intervention category (current reduction, temperature adjustment, SOC limitation) matches the literature-recommended strategy (i.e., action type match); (2) the predicted improvement direction is consistent with electrochemical expectations (i.e., direction correctness); and (3) the recommended parameter value falls within 50\% of the literature-optimal value (i.e., parameter proximity). Across the four scenarios, the framework achieved 100\% action-type match and direction correctness, but only 50\% parameter proximity due to our conservative safety margins that intentionally over-reduce current to minimize risk.

To provide quantitative ground-truth validation beyond comparison with literature guidelines, we conducted a retrospective \textit{natural experiment} using matched NASA battery pairs that were physically tested under different conditions but otherwise identical protocols. This follows the logic of observational causal inference \citep{pearl2009causality}: if our counterfactual simulator correctly predicts that changing condition $X$ would reduce degradation, then a real battery that actually operated under condition $X$ should exhibit a corresponding reduction.

We identified three matched pairs from the NASA Ames dataset: (1) B0029 at 43°C with 4A discharge vs.\ B0033 at 24°C with 4A discharge (same current, different temperature testing the Arrhenius-governed SEI prediction); (2) B0033 at 24°C with 4A discharge vs.\ B0036 at 24°C with 2A discharge (same temperature, different current—testing the mechanical-stress prediction); and (3) B0029 at 43°C/4A vs.\ B0005 at 24°C/2A (combined temperature and current effect). For each pair, we ran the counterfactual simulator on the harsher battery with the intervention ``change to the milder condition'' and compared the predicted degradation improvement against the actual degradation rate of the milder battery.

The simulator achieved \emph{100\% direction accuracy} (3/3 pairs): it correctly predicted that cooling B0029 from 43°C would reduce degradation (actual rate ratio: 1.38$\times$), that reducing B0033's discharge from 4A to 2A would slow capacity fade (actual ratio: 2.35$\times$), and that the combined intervention on B0029 would improve health (actual ratio: 1.44$\times$). The average ratio estimation error was 27.1\%, reflecting the simulator's conservative bias it consistently underestimates the magnitude of improvement while correctly predicting the direction. This conservative behavior is desirable in a safety-critical application where over-promising intervention benefits would be worse than under-promising them.

Consider a NASA battery pack operating in winter conditions: it is being fast-charged at 4°C, pushing 1.5C current to meet urgent power demands. The battery manager notices something troubling: capacity is dropping faster than expected.

An acceleration in degradation becomes visible around cycle 120, the "knee point" that signals trouble. \textbf{Panel (b)} presents HERO's zero-shot prediction. Despite never seeing this exact battery chemistry or operating condition during training, HERO retrieves three similar historical trajectories and predicts future degradation with 0.69\% error. The shaded blue region shows 95\% confidence bounds. \textbf{Panel (c)} reveals the culprit: our physics-informed causal model attributes 70\% of the damage to lithium plating, a phenomenon governed by the Butler-Volmer equation that becomes severe when charging cold batteries. \textbf{Panel (d)} explores "what if" scenarios. \emph{Stage 1: Prediction Without Prior Knowledge.} The battery manager uploads 168 discharge cycles of SOH measurements to HERO. The model has never seen this NASA chemistry or these cold conditions before. It was trained on entirely different cells. Yet by searching its memory bank of historical degradation patterns, HERO retrieves three similar trajectories (cells that also experienced accelerating degradation under stress) and predicts the next 25 cycles with remarkable accuracy: mean absolute error of just 0.69\%, R² of 0.990. This zero-shot transfer demonstrates that physics-constrained retrieval can generalize across battery types without retraining.

\emph{Stage 2: Diagnosis Through Physics.} HERO's prediction alone does not explain \emph{why} degradation is accelerating. The second stage runs a physics-informed causal analysis: for each of five degradation mechanisms (plating, SEI growth, active material loss, electrolyte decomposition, corrosion), a specialized neural network estimates its contribution while being constrained by known electrochemical laws. The verdict: lithium plating accounts for 70\% of capacity loss. This makes physical sense. At 4°C, lithium ions struggle to intercalate into graphite during fast charging, instead forming metallic deposits that permanently consume lithium inventory. The Butler-Volmer kinetics encoded in the model capture exactly this temperature-current interaction.

\emph{Stage 3: Counterfactual Reasoning.} Knowing that plating is the problem, what interventions would help? The counterfactual module simulates four scenarios. Under current conditions (baseline), the model predicts 70\% plating contribution will persist. Scenario A: reduce charging current from 1.5C to 1.0C. The physics model recalculates: plating drops to 10\% because lower current reduces overpotential at the anode. Scenario B: warm the battery to 15°C before charging. Plating falls to 5\%, as higher temperature improves lithium diffusion. Scenario C (optimal): combine both interventions. Result: 0\% plating risk, achieving complete elimination of the dominant degradation mode. This is not guesswork; these predictions are grounded in the same Butler-Volmer equations that govern the original attribution.

\emph{Stage 4: Context-Aware Guidance.} The final stage translates physics into action. PATT examines the battery's recent usage pattern and classifies it as "Cycling" (active use) rather than "Storage" with 99.2\% confidence. This context matters: a cycling battery needs different recommendations than one sitting idle. The advisory system generates two tiers. \emph{Tactical} (immediate actions): reduce charging current to 1.5A, preheat battery to 15°C before charging, monitor voltage depression as a plating indicator, alert if temperature drops below 5°C during charge. \emph{Strategic} (long-term planning): install a thermal management system, implement automatic C-rate limiting when temperature is below 10°C, store at 50\% SOC during periods of non-use, expected ROI of 22\% battery lifetime extension if thermal system is installed.
Taken together, these four stages create something greater than the sum of their parts. A battery manager does not receive isolated predictions or vague warnings. Instead, the operator receives a complete diagnostic narrative: "Your battery is degrading faster than normal (Stage 1). The cause is lithium plating from cold-weather fast charging (Stage 2). If you reduce current to 1.5A and preheat to 15°C, you can eliminate plating entirely (Stage 3). Here are specific actions to take today and infrastructure to plan for next quarter (Stage 4)." Each stage's output feeds the next, creating a coherent diagnostic and prescriptive pipeline grounded in electrochemical physics yet practical enough for field deployment.

\section{Discussion}

The power of our approach lies in the integration of components that are individually insufficient but collectively transformative. Prediction enables diagnosis; diagnosis enables intervention; intervention delivers life extension. Our explicit treatment of storage mode also addresses a critical gap in commercial BMS systems: most focus exclusively on cycling degradation, yet electric vehicles spend over 90\% of their time parked, meaning calendar aging often dominates real-world degradation. Our framework handles this through context-aware classification (PATT), physics-based constraints for storage scenarios, and SOC-dependent calendar aging models, achieving 99.2\% classification accuracy with 100\% cycling recall.

The limitations of the current system are described next. The memory bank spans five chemistries but remains dominated by lithium cobalt oxide (71\%) and nickel manganese cobalt (29\%) from the NASA, CALCE, Oxford, TJU, and XJTU datasets. Expanding coverage to lithium iron phosphate (LFP), high-nickel NMC variants (811), and eventually solid-state cells would improve generalization to the full range of commercial batteries.

Further our training data emphasizes cycling because that is what laboratory protocols measure. Calendar aging is harder to study as it requires waiting months or years rather than running accelerated tests. The 144-sample storage dataset we validated on helps, but dedicated multi-year aging studies spanning the full SOC and temperature space would substantially improve the SEI growth attribution.

Right now the system recommends but does not implement. Integration with vehicle BMS to automatically limit charging, trigger preheating, or adjust SOC targets based on upcoming parking duration would increase real-world impact. That requires partnerships with automakers and raises interesting questions about user control versus automatic optimization.

A practical concern for any battery management framework is real-time performance on resource-constrained hardware. Table~\ref{tab:inference_time} reports inference times for each pipeline component measured on a single-core Intel i7 CPU (no GPU required). The complete diagnostic pipeline, from raw measurements to actionable recommendations, executes in under 3 seconds, well within the decision window for real-time BMS applications. The memory bank requires approximately 1.3~MB of storage, and the Hybrid PINN contains 114,252 parameters (under 0.5~MB), making the framework deployable on embedded automotive controllers.

More broadly, this work offers a lesson for applied machine learning in physical sciences. Our ablation studies show that encoding established domain knowledge as soft physics constraints is not merely a performance trick; it is a fundamentally more data-efficient and generalizable learning strategy than purely data-driven approaches. The Hybrid PINN achieves 96.0\% accuracy on 75 benchmark scenarios with only 11,066 training samples, a scale where a pure deep learning approach would likely fail to generalize. The priors that enable this efficiency were distilled from decades of electrochemical research. As datasets in scientific domains remain small relative to internet-scale tasks, the path forward for applied ML is not to wait for more data, but to encode what we already know.

Our framework is complementary to recent work by \citet{gu2026realtime} on physics-informed neural networks for real-time SOH monitoring from partial charging profiles. While their P-PINNSPM approach embeds full single-particle model equations as hard constraints and demonstrates strong interpolation within a single LFP chemistry, our framework prioritizes cross-chemistry generalization through learned physics priors and retrieval-augmented prediction. The two approaches occupy different points on the physics-integration spectrum: P-PINNSPM uses \textit{hard} physical constraints (PDE residuals) for high-fidelity single-chemistry modeling, whereas our Hybrid PINN uses \textit{soft} physics priors for multi-chemistry disambiguation. A promising future direction would combine hard electrochemical constraints within each chemistry with our cross-chemistry retrieval and causal attribution framework.

\section{Conclusion}

We developed a machine learning system that solves three longstanding problems in battery health estimation: cross-chemistry generalization, causal mechanism attribution, and translation from prediction to prescription. The technical ML contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[(\alph*)]
    \item \emph{Retrieval-augmented dynamics:} Achieving 50-fold RUL improvement and 55\% error reduction on zero-shot chemistry transfer by storing and retrieving historical degradation trajectories
    \item \emph{Physics hybrid causal attribution:} Multi-head architecture with thermodynamic constraints achieving 96.0\% attribution accuracy. Ablation reveals a knowledge distillation effect: the neural network internalizes prior knowledge during training, achieving 93.3\% standalone, while the explicit priors provide a safety net for edge cases including cold-weather lithium plating
    \item \emph{Context-aware advisory:} Translating mechanism diagnoses into quantified user recommendations (e.g., 4$\times$ aging reduction from SOC management)
\end{enumerate}

The ablation study reveals a key insight for ML practitioners: physics priors serve as effective training-time regularizers that guide neural networks toward physically meaningful representations. At convergence, the network internalizes most prior knowledge, but the explicit priors remain essential as a safety net for rare, safety-critical scenarios. This dual role training regularizer and inference safety net suggests that encoding domain knowledge as soft constraints is valuable even when the model appears to have ``learned'' the knowledge independently.

This work demonstrates that some real-world problems need machine learning, but cannot be solved by machine learning alone. The path forward for applied ML in scientific domains is not bigger models or more data, but better integration of domain knowledge into learning algorithms. For battery management specifically, the 4$\times$ calendar aging reduction and 20\% life extension are achievable today with existing hardware; we just needed smarter algorithms.

For the 14 million EVs currently deployed and many more coming, this approach offers immediate practical value. The next step is deployment and field validation at scale.
